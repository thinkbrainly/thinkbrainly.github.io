12. Is rotational motion and torque are the same?


Answer:

No, rotational motion and torque are not the same.

Explanation:

Rotational motion refers to the circular motion of an object around a fixed axis, such as a wheel spinning on its axle.

Torque, on the other hand, is a measure of the force that causes an object to rotate around an axis. It is calculated as the product of the force applied to an object and the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the line of action of the force.

In other words, torque is what causes rotational motion, but rotational motion and torque are not interchangeable terms.


13. What are the conditions for equilibrium torque?


Answer:

Two conditions of equilibrium must be satisfied to ensure that an object remains in static equilibrium. Firstly, the net force acting upon the object must be zero. Secondly, the net torque acting upon the object must also be zero.

24. 30 Newton•meters of torque is required to close a door 1.5 meters wide. What force is needed to cause this torque?


Answer:

The force is 20N.

Explanation:

In our case, this problem is related to Moment of a Force. The moment here is also called torque which is the rotational effect to open the door.

For the formula, we are going to use

T = Fr

where

T     is the moment of a force or torque, unit is in N.m (but not Joules)

F     is the force, unit is in Newtons, N

r      is the distance perpendicular to the force from a pivot, unit is in meter

Given information

T = 30 N.m

r = 1.5 m

F = ?

Solving the problem

To solve for the force, use the formula for torque, we have:

T = Fr

30 N.m = F (1.5 m)

Divide both sides of the equation by 1.5 m, we have:

[tex]\frac{30N.m}{1.5m}=\frac{F}{1.5m}[/tex]

F = 20 N

Therefore, the force to open the door is 20N.

29. Is it possible (a) for a large force to produce a small, or even zero, torque and (b) for a small force to produce a large torque?


Torque is a rotational force to an object or twisting force in simple terms.
One good example is you and your friend riding a seesaw.

Torque must have a force (you and your friends' weight) and a moment arm or radius (in this case the length of the seesaw from the fulcrum/pivot to your position). Formula is T = F x r, where T is the Torque, F is force and r is radius.

To answer your questions:
(a) Yes it is possible to produce small/zero torque with a large force. In the seesaw case, the more you get near closer to the pivot or fulcrum, the smaller the torque no matter how heavy you are. Zero torque is possible if you sit in the pivot point, thus your radius is zero also.
(b) Yes again. Even though you have a small force, if your radius is large, it can directly affect your torque. In the seesaw case, try sitting on the end part of the seesaw, even if your weight (force) is small, it can greatly increase your torque.

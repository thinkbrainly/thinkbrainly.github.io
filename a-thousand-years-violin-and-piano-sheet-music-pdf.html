13. what you learned about • Music of the Romantic Period • Violin and Strings Music of Romantic Period • Piano Music of Romantic Music • Program Music​


Answer:

Music of the Romantic Period:

The Romantic Period in music roughly spanned from the late 18th century to the early 20th century, and it was characterized by a focus on individual expression, emotion, and freedom. Romantic composers often sought to convey intense personal feelings through their music, and they often used rich harmonies, complex melodies, and varied textures to achieve this. Some notable Romantic composers include Ludwig van Beethoven, Franz Schubert, Frederic Chopin, Franz Liszt, Johannes Brahms, Richard Wagner, and Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky.

Violin and Strings Music of Romantic Period:

The Romantic Period saw a great deal of innovation in violin and string music. Composers such as Niccolò Paganini and Pablo de Sarasate pushed the boundaries of what was thought possible on the violin, with virtuosic displays of technique and expression. String quartets also became increasingly popular, with composers such as Felix Mendelssohn and Antonín Dvořák writing works that emphasized the interplay between the four instruments. In general, the music of the Romantic Period placed a greater emphasis on expression and emotion than on technical precision, which allowed for a greater degree of freedom and experimentation.

Piano Music of Romantic Music:

The Romantic Period was a golden age for piano music, with a wide range of composers writing music for the instrument. Some of the most famous composers of Romantic piano music include Chopin, Liszt, Robert Schumann, Johannes Brahms, and Franz Schubert. The piano music of this period often featured complex harmonies, virtuosic displays of technique, and an emphasis on personal expression and emotion. Many composers also explored new forms and structures, such as the character piece, which was a short, evocative work that captured a particular mood or emotion.

Program Music:

Program music is a type of music that tells a story or conveys a particular mood or emotion, often through the use of descriptive titles or programmatic elements. This type of music became particularly popular during the Romantic Period, with composers such as Hector Berlioz, Richard Strauss, and Bedřich Smetana writing works that had a narrative or programmatic element. For example, Berlioz's "Symphonie fantastique" tells the story of an artist who falls in love with a woman and then experiences a series of increasingly wild and vivid hallucinations, while Smetana's "The Moldau" is a musical depiction of the river that flows through the composer's native Bohemia. Program music was a way for composers to explore and express their own personal experiences and emotions in a more concrete and explicit way than was possible with purely instrumental music.

Program music or programme music is a type of instrumental art music that attempts to render an extra-musical narrative musically. The narrative itself might be offered to the audience through the piece's title, or in the form of program notes, inviting imaginative correlations with the music. A well-known example is Hector Berlioz's Symphonie fantastique, an instrumental works which relates a series of morbid fantasies concerning the unrequited love of a sensitive poet involving murder, execution, and the torments of Hell.

The genre culminates in the symphonic works of Richard Strauss that include narrations of the adventures of Don Quixote, Till Eulenspiegel, the composer's domestic life, and an interpretation of Nietzsche's philosophy of the Übermensch. Following Strauss, the genre declined and new works with explicitly narrative content are rare. Nevertheless the genre continues to exert an influence on film music, especially where this draws upon the techniques of 19th century late romantic music.

The term is almost exclusively applied to works in the European classical music tradition, particularly those from the Romantic music period of the 19th century, during which the concept was popular, but pieces which fit the description have long been a part of music. The term is usually reserved for purely instrumental works (pieces without singers and lyrics), and not used, for example for opera or lieder.

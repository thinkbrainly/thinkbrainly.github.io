3. genetically modified organism is simply the product of gene editing. Agree or disagree?​


Genome editing, also known as gene editing, is a form of genetic engineering that produces genetically modified organisms (GMOs). Genome editing is a group of procedures that modifies the genetic make-up of bacteria, animals, and plants.

Gene editing is distinct from genetic modification because it does not result in the introduction of DNA from other species and produces new varieties similar to those that could be produced more slowly by natural breeding processes. However, they are currently regulated in the same way as genetically modified organisms.

What is genetically modified organism (GMO)?

An organism that has had its DNA altered through the use of genetic engineering methods is referred to as a genetically modified organism (GMO). Breeding techniques have been used by humans to alter creatures for thousands of years. Dogs, livestock, and even corn have all undergone years of selective breeding to develop specific desired features.

Foods that have been genetically modified (GM) are those that come from species whose genetic material (DNA) has been altered in a way that does not occur naturally, such as by introducing a gene from another organism.

DNA insertion into an organism's genome is a technique used in genetic engineering (GE). New DNA is inserted into plant cells to create a genetically modified plant. The cells are often cultured in tissue culture where they eventually become plants.

Genetically Modified Organisms

Popularly called as GMOs, they are animals, plants or microorganisms that had its DNA altered with the various techniques used in genetic engineering.

While the existence of these GMOs are controversial, they also play a helpful and beneficial role for both humanity and the world:

Examples:

1. Modified soybeans and corns

From being typical vegetables, these crops were genetically altered to have herbicide tolerance and insect resistance. In this way, farmers can conveniently grow them without much trouble. 40% of the total livestock in the United States consisting of soybeans and corn

2. Modified cotton

This kind of cotton can kill microorganisms and pests by producing internal insecticide which does not pose harm to humans. It is an ideal material for out lasting clothing. The use of cotton pesticides around the globe was approximately reduced to 90% after the creation of this cotton.

3. Papaya

The papaya industry was almost wiped out the United States in the late 20th century after the papaya ringspot virus spread like wild fire starting from Hawaii. With that in mind, a genetically engineered papaya, called the rainbow papaya, was developed and designed to resist the virus. Its seeds were distributed commercially to farmers. In just 10 years, 90% of papaya production in the States came from the rainbow papaya roots.

4. Hypoallergenic Cows

To reduce the risk of a number of people getting allergic reactions from milk, a cow named Daisy in New Zealand was genetically engineered to produce milk without the very protein which causes the allergy. This shows how humans can actually alter nutritional content of substances for a healthy cause, although the milk is still not for sale for the public.

During the 1900s, insulin is being extracted from animals inorder to treat diabetes. 26. identify and differentiate the different types of genetic analysis​


There are several types of genetic analysis, each with its own purpose and methods. Here are some of the most common types of genetic analysis:

1. Molecular genetics: This type of genetic analysis involves studying the structure and function of genes at the molecular level. This includes techniques such as DNA sequencing, gene editing, and gene expression analysis.

2. Cytogenetics: This type of genetic analysis involves studying the structure and function of chromosomes, including their number, size, and shape. This includes techniques such as karyotyping and fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH).

3. Genomic analysis: This type of genetic analysis involves studying the entire genome of an organism, including all of its genes and non-coding DNA. This includes techniques such as whole genome sequencing and genotyping.

4. Population genetics: This type of genetic analysis involves studying the genetic variation and evolution of populations over time. This includes techniques such as allele frequency analysis and phylogenetic analysis.

5. Epigenetics: This type of genetic analysis involves studying changes in gene expression and cellular phenotype that are not caused by changes in the underlying DNA sequence. This includes techniques such as DNA methylation analysis and histone modification analysis.

In summary, different types of genetic analysis are used to study different aspects of genetics, from the molecular structure of genes to the evolution of entire populations. Each type of analysis uses different techniques and methods to answer different questions about genetics.

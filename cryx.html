10. reflection Cry of Balintawak or Cry of Pugadlawin?​


The Pugadlawin Cry was the start of the Philippine Revolution against Spain. At the end of August 1896, members of the Katipunan secret society, called Katipuneros, led by Andrés Bonifacio rose up in a place called Caloocan, which may have extended into what is now Quezon City and was bigger than what is now Caloocan.

The "Cry of Pugadlawin" initiated the Philippine Revolution against Spain. The revolt grew in strength and spread to eight provinces, including Manila, Bulacan, Cavite, Pampanga, Tarlac, Laguna, Bata-ngas, and Nueva Ecija, which are represented by the eight sun rays on the current Philippine flag.

The main parts of "Cry of Pugadlawin" are:Andrés Bonifacio, who was in charge of the Katipunan, a secret revolutionary group that fought for the independence of the Philippines from Spanish colonial rule, is often credited with starting the Philippine Revolution.In August 1896, the Katipuneros, led by Andrés Bonifacio, rebelled in the sitio of Pugadlawin in Balintawak, which is now a part of Quezon City. They did this by tearing up their "cedulas," which became a symbol of the Filipino people's slavery.The "Cry of Pugadlawin" is officially recognized as the start of the use of force and weapons in the Philippine Revolution against Spain.

11. Compare and Contrast Cry of Balintawak and Cry of Pugadlawin


The Cry of Balintawak and the Cry of Pugadlawin are two significant events in Philippine history that marked the beginning of the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule. Here are some comparisons and contrasts between the two:

Similarities:

- Both events were sparked by the desire of Filipinos to fight for their independence from Spanish colonial rule.

- Both events were led by Filipino revolutionaries who were inspired by the ideals of the Propaganda Movement and the injustices of Spanish colonial rule.

- Both events involved the raising of a Philippine flag as a symbol of Filipino nationalism and resistance against Spanish colonialism.

Differences:

- The Cry of Balintawak happened on August 26, 1896, while the Cry of Pugadlawin happened on August 23, 1896, three days earlier.

- The Cry of Balintawak was led by a woman, Gregoria de Jesus, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was led by Andres Bonifacio, the founder of the Katipunan.

- The Cry of Balintawak was a peaceful protest, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was a violent uprising that marked the start of the armed struggle against Spanish colonial rule.

- The Cry of Balintawak was a localized event that took place in Caloocan, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was a more widespread event that involved several towns in the province of Manila.

- The Cry of Balintawak was a response to the arrest of Filipino nationalist leaders, while the Cry of Pugadlawin was a premeditated revolt that aimed to overthrow Spanish colonial rule.

Overall, both the Cry of Balintawak and the Cry of Pugadlawin were significant events that played a crucial role in the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonialism. While they had some similarities, they also had some notable differences in terms of their timing, leadership, nature, and goals.

Dahil sa magkakaibang pahayag at kalabuan ng lugar kung saan nangyari ito, ang tumpak na petsa at lugar ng sigaw ay pinagtatalunan pa.[2][3] Mula 1908 hanggang 1963, ang opisyal na paninindigan ay nangyari ang sigaw noong Agosto 26 sa Balintawak.

Answer:

Ossicles

Explanation:

The ossicles (also called auditory ossicles) are three bones in either middle ear that are among the smallest bones in the human body. They serve to transmit sounds from the air to the fluid-filled labyrinth (cochlea). The absence of the auditory ossicles would constitute a moderate-to-severe hearing loss. The term "ossicle" literally means "tiny bone". Though the term may refer to any small bone throughout the body, it typically refers to the malleus, incus, and stapes (hammer, anvil, and stirrup) of the middle ear.

What Are Ears and What Do They Do?

The ear is made up of three different sections that work together to collect sounds and send them to the brain: the outer ear, the middle ear, and the inner ear.

Explanation:

Outer Ear: Collecting Sounds

The outer ear is made up of the pinna — also called the auricle (say: OR-ih-kul) — and the ear canal. The pinna is the part of the ear you see on the side of your head. It's made of tough cartilage covered by skin. Its main job is to gather sounds and funnel them to the ear canal, which is the pathway that leads to the middle ear. Glands in the skin lining the ear canal make earwax, which protects the canal by cleaning out dirt and helping to prevent infections.Middle Ear: Good VibrationsThe middle ear is an air-filled cavity that turns sound waves into vibrations and delivers them to the inner ear. The middle ear is separated from the outer ear by the eardrum, or tympanic (say: tim-PAN-ik) membrane, a thin piece of tissue stretched tight across the ear canal. Sounds hit the eardrum, making it move.

This movement leads to vibrations of three very small bones in the middle ear known as the ossicles (say: AH-sih-kuls). The ossicles are:

the malleus (say: MAH-lee-us) ("hammer"), which is attached to the eardrumthe incus (say: IN-kus) ("anvil"), which is attached to the malleusthe stapes (say: STAY-peez) ("stirrup"), which is attached to the incus and is the smallest bone in the bodyHope it helps Pa brainliest nalang po thanks

25. Give the meaning/ use or Functions of the parts of the Ear.1. Outer Ear-2. Middle Ear-3. Inner Ear-4. Eardrum-5. Cochlea- 6. Pinna- 7. Stirrup-8. Hammer-9. Anvil- 10. Ear Canal-​


Answer:

Outer Ear: The outer ear, which includes the pinna, is the visible part of the ear that collects and directs sound waves towards the ear canal. It also helps to localize the source of sound.

Middle Ear: The middle ear is an air-filled cavity behind the eardrum. It contains three small bones, the hammer, anvil, and stirrup, which vibrate in response to sound waves transmitted from the eardrum. The middle ear also serves to amplify and equalize sound waves before they are transmitted to the inner ear.

Inner Ear: The inner ear consists of the cochlea, which is responsible for hearing, and the vestibular system, which helps with balance and spatial orientation. The cochlea contains thousands of tiny hair cells that convert sound waves into electrical signals that are transmitted to the brain via the auditory nerve.

Eardrum: The eardrum, or tympanic membrane, is a thin, flexible membrane that separates the outer ear from the middle ear. When sound waves hit the eardrum, it vibrates, causing the bones in the middle ear to vibrate as well.

Cochlea: The cochlea is a spiral-shaped organ in the inner ear that contains tiny hair cells that convert sound waves into electrical signals that are sent to the brain via the auditory nerve.

Pinna: The pinna, also known as the auricle, is the visible part of the outer ear that helps to collect and direct sound waves towards the ear canal.

Stirrup: The stirrup, also known as the stapes, is the smallest bone in the human body. It is one of the three bones in the middle ear that transmit sound waves from the eardrum to the cochlea.

Hammer: The hammer, also known as the malleus, is one of the three bones in the middle ear that transmit sound waves from the eardrum to the cochlea.

Anvil: The anvil, also known as the incus, is one of the three bones in the middle ear that transmit sound waves from the eardrum to the cochlea.

Ear Canal: The ear canal, also known as the auditory canal, is the tube-like structure that connects the outer ear to the middle ear. It helps to amplify sound waves and protect the delicate structures of the middle ear.

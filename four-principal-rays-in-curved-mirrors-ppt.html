16. what are the four principal rays used to draw ray diagram​


Principal Rays

The ray that comes from an off-axis object point and travels through the middle of the stop is known as the principal ray. The major ray also passes through the center of the entrance and exit pupils when there are no aberrations in the pupils. Use a straight edge to accurately draw one ray on the ray diagram so that it precisely passes through the focus point and moves in the direction of the mirror. In order for the second ray to propagate exactly parallel to the main axis, it must be drawn in this manner. To show which way the rays are moving, place arrows over them.

The four main rays are used to draw a ray diagram:

1. Ray diagrams for lenses

Three main rays can be used to find and size a picture created by a single lens. Examples are provided for lenses that are divergent and convergent, as well as for situations in which the object is both inside and outside of the major focal length.

2. Ray diagrams for convex lenses

An inverted real image will be created for things outside the focal point, while an upright virtual image will be created for objects inside the focal point.

3. Ray diagrams for concave lenses

Similar outcomes are produced by concave lenses inside and outside of the focal point: an upright virtual image that is smaller than the object. The focal length of the lens is where the image is always created.

4. Ray diagram for two lenses

After refraction, parallel rays collide at the focus, and parallel rays that have already passed through the focus recombine.

Answer:

In the analysis of image formation in curved mirrors, the four principal rays are used to determine the location, size, and nature of the image. These four rays are:

The incident ray parallel to the principal axis: This ray reflects off the mirror and passes through the focal point on the opposite side of the mirror.

The incident ray passing through the center of curvature: This ray reflects off the mirror and retraces its path, remaining parallel to the principal axis.

The incident ray passing through the focal point: This ray reflects off the mirror and emerges parallel to the principal axis.

The incident ray that is reflected by the mirror: This ray reflects off the mirror and converges at the focal point on the same side of the mirror.

Using these four principal rays, it is possible to determine the location, size, and nature of the image formed by a curved mirror. For example, a concave mirror forms a real, inverted image that is smaller than the object when the object is placed between the focal point and the center of curvature. On the other hand, a convex mirror forms a virtual, erect and smaller image regardless of where the object is placed.

It's worth noting that these four principal rays are used as a simplification to understand the concepts of image formation, in reality the image formation is determined by all the light rays coming from the object and reflecting off the mirror.

27. what are the four principal rays on concave and convex mirrors​


Answer:

In general, from any point which is not itself on “the principal axis” of the mirror

a) a ray incident at “the pole” is reflected back at an equal angle, the other side of the axis.

b) a ray incident parallel to the principal axis reflects back towards the focal point of a concave mirror, or in the case of a convex mirror, along the straight line from the focal point to the point of incidence.

c) a ray incident towards the focal point of a convex mirror, or along a line from the focal point of a cocave mirror, is reflected back parallel to the principal axis.

d) a radial ray, i.e. travelling along the radius towards the focal point of a convex mirror, or away from the focal point of a concave mirror, reflects back along its original path.

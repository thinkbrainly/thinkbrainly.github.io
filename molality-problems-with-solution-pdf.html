7. The vapor pressure of water at 30°C is 31.8 mmHg. Compare the vapor pressure of a 1 molal solution of NaCl and a 1 molal solution of glucose in water. With solution.ASAP!! Thankyouu!​


Answer:

The vapor pressure of a solution is lower than the vapor pressure of the pure solvent due to the presence of solute molecules in the solution. This phenomenon is known as vapor pressure lowering.

The extent of vapor pressure lowering depends on the concentration and nature of the solute. For a dilute solution, the lowering of vapor pressure can be approximated by Raoult's law, which states that the vapor pressure of a solution is proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent in the solution:

P = X_solvent * P_solvent

where P is the vapor pressure of the solution, X_solvent is the mole fraction of the solvent, and P_solvent is the vapor pressure of the pure solvent.

For a 1 molal solution, the mole fraction of the solvent is approximately 0.99 (assuming the molar volume of the solute is negligible compared to that of the solvent). Therefore, the vapor pressure of the solution can be calculated as:

P = 0.99 * P_water

where P_water is the vapor pressure of pure water at the given temperature.

For the given temperature of 30°C, the vapor pressure of water is 31.8 mmHg. Therefore, the vapor pressure of a 1 molal solution of NaCl and a 1 molal solution of glucose can be calculated as follows:

For NaCl:

P = 0.99 * 31.8 mmHg = 31.5 mmHg

For glucose:

P = 0.99 * 31.8 mmHg = 31.5 mmHg

Therefore, the vapor pressure of both solutions is almost identical and is lower than the vapor pressure of pure water.


8. The vapor pressure of water at 30°C is 31.8 mmHg. Compare the vapor pressure of a 1 molal solution of NaCl and a 1 molal solution of glucose in water​


The vapor pressure of a solution is given by the equation:

ΔP = Xsolute × P°solute × mole fraction of solvent

Where ΔP is the lowering of vapor pressure, Xsolute is the mole fraction of the solute, P°solute is the vapor pressure of the pure solute, and the mole fraction of the solvent is approximately 1 since it is in large excess.

For a 1 molal solution of NaCl, the mole fraction of NaCl is 1/(1+55.5) = 0.0177 (assuming complete dissociation). The vapor pressure lowering is then:

ΔP(NaCl) = 0.0177 × 23.8 mmHg (vapor pressure of NaCl at 30°C) ≈ 0.42 mmHg

For a 1 molal solution of glucose, the mole fraction of glucose is 1/(1+180) = 0.0055. The vapor pressure lowering is then:

ΔP(glucose) = 0.0055 × 31.8 mmHg (vapor pressure of water at 30°C) ≈ 0.175 mmHg

Therefore, the vapor pressure of the 1 molal solution of NaCl will be lower than that of the 1 molal solution of glucose.

9. What is the molal concentration of 30 % ethanol solution (c2h5oh)?

Explanation:

Molality (m) is defined as moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. On the other hand, molarity (M) is a unit of concentration which has a unit of moles of solute per liters of solution.

Let us keep these in mind as we go further to the solution for this problem.

Given:

5.86 M (molar) of ethanol (C₂H₅OH)

Required:

Molality (m)

Solution:

We assume 1 liters of solution for this problem.

Get first the molar mass (MM) of the ethanol.

MM ethanol = (12.012 g/mol*2) + (1.008*5) + (15.999) + (1.008)

MM ethanol = 46.071 g/mol

Getting the mass of ethanol from 5.86 M, we have,

5.86 moles ethanol (since we assume 1 L of solution)

5.86 moles ethanol * 46.071 g/mol = 269.9761 grams ethanol

Getting the mass of solution from the density, we have,

0.927 g/mL * 1000 mL = 927 grams solution

(We used multiplier 1000 mL because 1 L = 1000 mL)

Solving for the mass of solvent, we have,

mass of solvent = mass of solution - mass of solute

mass of solvent = 927 grams - 269.9761 grams

mass of solvent = 657.024 grams solvent = 0.657024 kilograms solvent

Solving for molality, we have,

m = moles solute ÷ kilograms of solvent

m = 5.86 moles of ethanol ÷ 0.657024 kg solvent

m = 8.919

Therefore, the molality of the solution is 8.919 molal.

13. Calculate the molal concentration of 40% ethanol solution (c2h5oh).


SOLUTION:

Step 1: List the given values.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & MM_{\text{solute}} = \text{46.069 g/mol} \\ & \%(m/m) = 40\% \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mass of solute (ethanol).

Assume that the mass of solution is 100 g.

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{\%(m/m)}{100\%} \times mass_{\text{solution}} \\ & = \frac{40\%}{100\%} \times \text{100 g} \\ & = \text{40 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\begin{aligned} n_{\text{solute}} & = \frac{mass_{\text{solute}}}{MM_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{40 g}}{\text{46.069 g/mol}} \\ & = \text{0.86826 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the mass of solvent.

[tex]\begin{aligned} mass_{\text{solvent}} & = mass_{\text{solution}} - mass_{\text{solute}} \\ & = \text{100 g} - \text{40 g} \\ & = \text{60 g} \\ & = \text{0.0600 kg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m & = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{mass_{\text{solvent}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.86826 mol}}{\text{0.0600 kg}} \\ & = \text{14.5 mol/kg} \\ & = \boxed{14.5 \: m} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molal concentration of 40% ethanol solution is 14.5 m.

15. The vapor pressure of water at 30°C is 31.8 mmHg. Compare the vapor pressure of a 1 molal solution of NaCl and a 1 molal solution of glucose in water


The vapor pressure of a solution is lower than the vapor pressure of the pure solvent. This is known as Raoult's law. The extent to which the vapor pressure is lowered depends on the concentration of the solute in the solution.

Assuming ideal behavior, the vapor pressure of a 1 molal solution of NaCl can be calculated using the following equation:

P = X_solvent * P^o_solvent

where P is the vapor pressure of the solution, X_solvent is the mole fraction of water in the solution, and P^o_solvent is the vapor pressure of pure water. Since the solution is 1 molal, the mole fraction of water is 0.9901 (assuming the density of the solution is 1 g/mL). Thus:

P_NaCl = 0.9901 * 31.8 mmHg = 31.5 mmHg

Similarly, the vapor pressure of a 1 molal solution of glucose can be calculated using the same equation:

P_glucose = X_solvent * P^o_solvent

The mole fraction of water in this solution is 0.9901 as well, since glucose does not significantly dissociate in water. Thus:

P_glucose = 0.9901 * 31.8 mmHg = 31.5 mmHg

Therefore, the vapor pressure of both solutions is approximately the same, since they have the same concentration and do not significantly dissociate in water.

20. what is the molality of a solution containing 0.780g ammonia in 137g of water?​


Molality (m)Discussion:

Molality is one of the common units of concentration of a solution. Molality is defined as the number of moles of solute dissolved in 1 kg (1000 g) of solvent --- that is,

[tex]\boxed{\text{molality} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}}}[/tex]

For example, to prepare a 1 molal, or 1 [tex]m[/tex], sodium sulfate (Na₂SO₄) aqueous solution, we need to dissolve 1 mol (142.0 g) of the substance in 1000 g (1 kg) of water. Depending on the nature of the solute-solvent interaction, the final volume of the solution will be either greater than or less than 1000 mL. It is also possible, though very unlikely, that the final volume could be equal to 1000 mL.

[tex]\\[/tex]

Problem:

What is the molality of a solution containing 0.780 g ammonia in 137 g of water?

Given:

[tex]\text{mass of solute = 0.780 g}[/tex]

[tex]\text{mass of solvent = 137 g = 0.137 kg}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solute} = \text{NH}_{3}[/tex]

[tex]\text{solvent} = \text{H}_{2}\text{O}[/tex]

Required:

[tex]\text{molality}[/tex]

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of solute.

molar mass of solute = (14.01 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 3)

molar mass of solute = 17.034 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute.

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{molar mass of solute}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute} = \frac{\text{0.780 g}}{\text{17.034 g/mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of solute = 0.045791 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the molality of solution.

[tex]\text{molality} = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent (kg)}}[/tex]

24. Calculate the molality of 5.86molar ethanol solution whose density is 0.927


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Determine the number of moles of solute (ethanol) and the volume of solution.

Since the given concentration is 5.86 M, there are 5.86 moles of solute in 1 L of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & \text{moles of solute} = \text{5.86 mol} \\ & \text{volume of solution} = \text{1 L = 1000 mL} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mass of solute.

The molar mass of ethanol is 46.07 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solute} & = \text{moles of solute} \times \text{molar mass of solute} \\ & = \text{5.86 mol} \times \text{46.07 g/mol} \\ & = \text{270. g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mass of solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solution} & = \text{density of solution} \times \text{volume of solution} \\ & = \text{0.927 g/mL} \times \text{1000 mL} \\ & = \text{927 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 4: Calculate the mass of solvent in kilograms.

Note that 1 kg = 1000 g

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of solvent} & = \text{mass of solution} - \text{mass of solute} \\ & = \text{927 g} - \text{270. g} \\ & = \text{657 g} \\ & = \text{0.657 kg} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 5: Calculate the molality of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molality} & = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent}} \\ & = \frac{\text{5.86 mol}}{\text{0.657 kg}} \\ & = \text{8.92 mol/kg} \\ & = \boxed{8.92 \: m} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the molality of the solution is 8.92 m.

28. 100.0 g of saltwater is weighed out and all of the water evaporated. The remaining salt is found to have a mass of 23.8 g. What was the molality of the original solution? (Hint: Be sure to check the definition of molality before doing this problem!)


Answer:

5.34 mol/kg or 5.34 m

Explanation:

Molality Problem

Molality (m) is defined as the moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. The main difference between molarity and molality is that molarity deals with volume of solution (in liters) while molality deals with the mass of solvent (in kilograms). Mathematically, molality can be written as:

[tex]\displaystyle m=\frac{\rm{moles\;of\;solute}}{\rm{kilogram\;of\;solvent}}[/tex]

In the problem, we are given with 100.0 g of saltwater which is the mass of the solution and 23.8 grams of salt which is the mass of solute.

To find the mass of solvent, we need to subtract the mass of solute from the mass of solution.

mass of solvent = mass of solution - mass of solute

mass of solvent = 100.0 grams - 23.8 grams

mass of solvent = 76.2 grams

Next, we need to calculate for the moles of solute. Assuming that the solute is table salt (NaCl), the molar mass of NaCl is 58.44 g/mol.

[tex]\displaystyle \rm{moles\;NaCl} = \frac{23.8\;\rm{grams\;NaCl}}{58.44\;\frac{g}{mol}}\\\\\rm{moles\;NaCl} = 0.407\;\rm{moles}[/tex]

Since we now have 0.407 moles of solute, we can now solve for molality (m).

[tex]\displaystyle m=\frac{\rm{moles\;of\;solute}}{\rm{kilogram\;of\;solvent}}=\frac{0.407 \;\rm{moles}}{{76.2\;\rm{grams}}\times\frac{1\;\rm{kg}}{1000\;\rm{grams}}}\\\\m=5.34\;\rm{\frac{mol}{kg}}[/tex]

Thus, the molality of the original solution is 5.34 m.

30. Molality from weight percent: What is the molality of an 18.0% aqueous solution of KCl?


Molality is the amount of substance in moles of the solute, divided by the mass in kg of the solvent.

Now, in this case, what we need is moles KCl, divided by the mass in kg of the solvent.

We are not given the mass of the solvent, but we are given that the solution is 18% aqueous solution of KCl. This means that for every 100g water, there is 18g KCl. Since we want our solvent to be in kg, we say that there is 180g KCl for every 1000g water, or 1 kg water.

Now, we have to convert the 180g KCl into moles. Based on the periodic table, the weights are:

K: 39.10 g/mol

Cl: 35.45 g/mol

Adding these two:

KCl has 39.10 + 35.45 = 74.55 g/mol

then 180 g KCl x (1 mol KCl/ 74.55g KCl) = 2.41 mol KCl

Now we just divide 2.41 mol KCl by 1 kg H2O = 2.41 molal

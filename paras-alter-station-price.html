Answer:

Altering of clothes refers to the process of making changes to an existing piece of clothing to better fit the wearer's body or style preferences. This can involve taking in or letting out seams, hemming or shortening sleeves, adjusting the length or width of a garment, or adding embellishments or decorations.

The economic value of alteration lies in its ability to extend the life of a garment, making it more cost-effective and sustainable for the wearer. Instead of buying a new piece of clothing, altering an existing garment can be a more affordable and environmentally friendly option. Alteration can also save money by allowing people to purchase clothing that may not fit perfectly off the rack, knowing that they can have it altered to fit their body.

The social value of alteration is also significant. Clothing that fits properly can enhance a person's confidence and self-esteem, making them feel more comfortable and presentable in social situations. Alteration can also help people to express their personal style, allowing them to customize their clothing to their own preferences and tastes.

Additionally, alteration can provide job opportunities and income for individuals and small businesses specializing in sewing and alterations. By supporting local tailors and seamstresses, communities can help to sustain a valuable craft and keep traditional textile skills alive.

25. 6. A television station is considering the sale of promotional videos. It can have the videos produced by one of two suppliers. Supplier A will charge the station a set-up fee of $1,200 plus $2 for each DVD; supplier B has no set-up fee and will charge $4 per DVD. The station estimates its demand for the DVDs to be given by Q =1,600 -200P, where P is the price in dollars and Q is the number of DVDs. (The price equation is P= 8 - Q/200.) a. Suppose the station plans to give away the videos. How many DVDs should it order? From which supplier? b. Suppose instead that the station seeks to maximize its profit from sales of the DVDs. What price should it charge? How many DVDs should it order from which supplier? (Hint: Solve two separate problems, one with supplier A and one with supplier B, and then compare profits. In each case, apply the MR = MC rule.)


a. If the station plans to give away the DVDs, the price will be zero. Substituting zero for P in the demand equation, we get Q = 1,600, so the station should order 1,600 DVDs. The total cost for supplier A will be $1,200 for the setup fee plus $2 * 1,600 = $3,200 for the DVDs, for a total cost of $4,400. The total cost for supplier B will be $4 * 1,600 = $6,400. Therefore, the station should choose supplier A.

b. To maximize its profit, the station should set the price such that marginal revenue (MR) equals marginal cost (MC). The marginal cost for supplier A is $2 per DVD, and the marginal cost for supplier B is $4 per DVD.

For supplier A:

The demand equation is Q = 1,600 - 200P, so the marginal revenue is MR = -200 * P. Setting MR = MC, we get -200P = 2. Solving for P, we find that the optimal price is P = $0.01. Substituting this value for P in the demand equation, we find that the optimal quantity is Q = 1,600 - 200 * 0.01 = 1,600 DVDs. The total revenue will be P * Q = $0.01 * 1,600 = $16, and the total cost will be $1,200 for the setup fee plus $2 * 1,600 = $3,200 for the DVDs, for a total cost of $4,400. The profit will be $16 - $4,400 = -$4,384.

For supplier B:

Setting MR = MC, we get -200P = 4. Solving for P, we find that the optimal price is P = $0.02. Substituting this value for P in the demand equation, we find that the optimal quantity is Q = 1,600 - 200 * 0.02 = 1,600 DVDs. The total revenue will be P * Q = $0.02 * 1,600 = $32, and the total cost will be $4 * 1,600 = $6,400. The profit will be $32 - $6,400 = -$6,368.

Therefore, the station should not sell the DVDs because it will not be able to cover its costs and will incur a loss in either case.

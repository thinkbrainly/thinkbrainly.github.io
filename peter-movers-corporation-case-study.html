2. The bankruptcy of enron corporation in 2001 case study solution


Answer:

Enron's Energy Origins

Enron was formed in 1985 following a merger between Houston Natural Gas Company and Omaha-based InterNorth Incorporated. Following the merger, Kenneth Lay, who had been the chief executive officer (CEO) of Houston Natural Gas, became Enron's CEO and chair. Lay quickly rebranded Enron into an energy trader and supplier. Deregulation of the energy markets allowed companies to place bets on future prices, and Enron was poised to take advantage. In 1990, Lay created the Enron Finance Corporation and appointed Jeffrey Skilling, whose work as a McKinsey & Company consultant had impressed Lay, to head the new corporation. Skilling was then one of the youngest partners at McKinsey.4

Skilling joined Enron at an auspicious time. The era's minimal regulatory environment allowed Enron to flourish. At the end of the 1990s, the dot-com bubble was in full swing, and the Nasdaq hit 5,000.5 Revolutionary internet stocks were being valued at preposterous levels and, consequently, most investors and regulators simply accepted spiking share prices as the new normal.

Northern Container Corporation: Time to Cry

Only five years into business, his organization is looking for new clients who have the potential to work with him on an ongoing basis.

NCC produces ordinary and sanitary tin cans, has facilities for filling cooking oil, and fabricates molds and dies. Its factory and corporate headquarters are in Valenzuela, Metro Manila.

Engineer Monsod has been putting a lot of effort into getting NCC operating at full capacity ever since its first year of operation. There are many employees working in the company's many departments, and that number is increasing by annually.

The rising customer demand for the company's products has led to an increase in the number of employees. However, according to Engineer Monsod, the business just needs one more satisfied customer to reach full operation.

Engineer Monsod first spoke with Mr. Godefredo Tapiador, the general manager of a recently established business, in October 1996. Mr. Tapiador was persuaded to order his tin can requirements from NCC by Engineer Monsod.

On January 10, 1997, NCC's production manager informed engineer Monsod that unless his division receives fresh manufacturing orders, he will be compelled to recommend the layoff of 12 factory employees. 12 employees were actually let go five days later.

29. 2 prime movers of globalization


Answer:

Modern economic globalization would be impossible without our ability to move billions of tonnes of raw materials and finished goods among the continents and to fly at speeds approaching the speed of sound. These realities were made possible by the interaction of economic and technical factors. Much has been written about their organizational and political underpinnings (ranging from the role of multinational corporations to the history of free trade agreements), but much less on the history of the two prime movers that made these realities possible. Neither steam engines, nor gasoline-fuelled engines could have accomplished comparable feats. Diesel engines made ocean shipping the cheapest mode of long-distance transport and without gas turbines there would be no fast, inexpensive, mass-scale intercontinental travel. This paper examines the history, advances, benefits and costs of the two prime movers

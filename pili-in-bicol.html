7. Alden went to Bicol and bought 25 packs of pili nuts for Php875 with 10% discount. When he got back to Manila, he sold 15 packs for Php675, and the rest at Php40 each. How much profit did Alden gain?


Answer:

Alden gained a total of ₱112.5 profit

The 10% discount was ₱87.5, so the total price of the pili nuts that Alden bought was ₱962.5. Then, he sold the 15 packs of pili nuts for ₱675, so there will be 10 remaining packs of pili nuts that he sold for ₱40 each.

40 × 10 = ₱400

400 + 675 = ₱1,075

1,075 - 962.5 = ₱112.5

8. How can "Pagtilad" culture influence the development of the pili nut: crown jewel bicol​.


Answer:

the traditional de-shelling of the Pili Nut, a legend in itself, is an experiential attraction within the Pili Nut industry. Experts throughout the Bicol Region practice the centuries-old process of pagtilad (that is, Pili Nut de-shelling using a bolo and a wooden board) with precision, speed and strength unrivaled even by modern-day machines.

De-shelling a Pili Nut is an epic case of Man versus Machine, where Man refreshingly wins. Pili deshelling machines are not quite successful as they do crush the extremely hard, bony shell, but unfortunately tend to crush the precious kernel as well, in statistics too high for commercial viability.

Retrieving a perfect Pili kernel requires precision only human hands can deliver; this is easily due to the centuries-old tradition of Pili-cracking, enough practice to beat any machine. The Bicolanos call the process pagtilad – cracking the tough nut using a bolo, with unbelievably rhythmic, graceful and accurate whacking. A paratilad expert could easily slice through the thick, hardwood-esque Pili shells, and finish 100 kilograms in a day’s work.

20. why is bicol known as bicol volcanic arc​


The Bicol region, located in the southeastern part of Luzon island in the Philippines, is known as the Bicol Volcanic Arc because it is an active volcanic chain composed of numerous stratovolcanoes and volcanic edifices.

The Bicol Volcanic Arc is part of the Pacific Ring of Fire, an area in the Pacific Ocean where many earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occur due to the convergence of several tectonic plates. The subduction of the Philippine Sea Plate under the Eurasian Plate has resulted in the formation of the volcanic arc, with magma rising from the Earth's mantle and causing volcanic activity.

The Bicol Volcanic Arc consists of several active and inactive volcanoes, including Mayon Volcano, one of the most active and iconic volcanoes in the Philippines. Other notable volcanoes in the area include Bulusan Volcano, Mount Iriga, Mount Masaraga, and Mount Malinao.

The volcanic activity in the Bicol region has contributed to the fertility of its soil and the abundance of natural resources, including geothermal energy. However, it also poses risks to the local communities, as volcanic eruptions can cause damage to infrastructure, crops, and homes, as well as put the lives of people in danger.

Overall, the Bicol Volcanic Arc is a significant natural feature of the Philippines and a source of both opportunities and challenges for the people and communities living in the area.

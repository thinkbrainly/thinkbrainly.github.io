Permission, Obligation, Prohibition

The answer to the questions are:

MUST - OBLIGATIONHAS TO - OBLIGATIONMAY - PERMISSIONCAN'T - PROHIBITIONHAVE TO - OBLIGATIONMUST NOT - PROHIBITIONCAN - PERMISSIONWILL - OBLIGATIONSHOULD - OBLIGATION

Further explanation:

Usual modal verb forms include permission, prohibition, obligation, and no obligation.

Permission

To provide permission, use the words can and may. In ordinary circumstances, may be less frequent and more formal:

If you like, you can use my automobile.With adult supervision, children may use the pool.

Use the words can, could, and may to request permission. Can is the most direct, could is more cautious and formal, and may is the most formal of the three.

Could I borrow your phone?Could you please give me a drink of water?May I begin my presentation?

Might, which is more ambiguous, is extremely formal and infrequently used:

Might I ask that question to you?

In the past, it was possible to convey widespread permission:

When I was a kid, I could do pretty much whatever I wanted.

However, when referring to a specific activity that was authorized and carried out, we use the following verb forms:

I was allowed to participate in the race even though I was underage.

Prohibition

To convey prohibitions, the negative terms cannot/cannot, mustn't, and may not (more formal) are used:

You cannot stay here, I'm sorry.That Ming vase must not be handled.Members of the public may not vote on matters before the committee.

When used negatively, the verb "be to" can also convey restrictions:

You are not to repeat that!

When discussing permission without really granting, rejecting, or requesting it, we may use the words can, can't, or be permitted to

For their project, students can choose any topic they like.In the library, we cannot eat.Is smoking permitted in this restaurant?Will reporters be allowed to submit questions?

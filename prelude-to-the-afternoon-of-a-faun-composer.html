29. Listen to the composition of Claude Debussy, Prelude to the Afternoon of a Faun. And make a reaction paper. (20pts)


Answer:

Claude DebussyPrelude to the Afternoon of a  Faun

Prelude to the Afternoon of a Faun is a musical interpretation of Stéphane Mallarmé's poem "Afternoon of a Faun," in which a faun—an ancient Greek fable of a half-man, half-goat creature—wakes to luxuriate in sensual memories of forest nymphs.

Debussy starts the piece with a sinuous flute tune that evokes a delicate female shape. The strings, harp, and horns play gently swelling notes soon after. The music progresses without abrupt changes; motifs mix into one another, rising and dropping gradually. Clarinet and oboe solos appear in the middle section before the flute gradually reclaims the spotlight. Airy traces of percussion from finger cymbals can be heard in the concluding moments.

Prelude to a Faun's Afternoon is a masterwork of timbral composition. By late-nineteenth-century norms, Debussy employs a relatively modest orchestra. Brass and percussion are almost entirely absent. The only percussion is crotales, which are utilized sparingly and well. The only brass instruments are four ho rns, with a third flute and an English ho rn in the wind section.

This prelude's music is a free interpretation of Mallarmé's lovely text. It makes no claim to being a synthesis of it. Rather, there is a series of sequences in which the faun's wishes and dreams are expressed in the heat of the afternoon. Then, exhausted from chasing nymphs and naiads, he falls into a hypnotic sleep, in which he can finally realize his aspirations of possessing universal Nature.

Mallarmé's poem, about "a faun dreaming of the conquering of nymphs," blurs the lines between dream and reality, providing Debussy with the ideal setting in which to experiment with his new language. Prelude is regarded as a watershed moment in music history, with far-reaching ramifications for subsequent generations of composers. Debussy had developed an immensely original style, both in terms of orchestration and in his approach to harmony and musical structure. Debussy discovered the ideal technique to capture the dream state of the faun's afternoon in this way.
